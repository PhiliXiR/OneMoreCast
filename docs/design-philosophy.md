# One More Cast Design Philosophy

One More Cast is a fishing RPG in which learning a particular body of water is
the adventure. Fishing is not a break between the meaningful parts of the game:
it is how the player explores, learns, helps people, progresses, and encounters
the watershed mystery.

## The compact foundation

The first complete game area is the home water: one continuous lake around a
small community. It must be rewarding as a self-contained experience and also
establish content and system patterns that can later extend into a full
watershed. We earn expansion through density, not map size.

The home water contains three authored micro-habitats:

- working dock and shallows;
- vegetated inlet;
- deeper or rockier far bank.

Each micro-habitat must be recognizable by shape and memory, not by a map icon.
It must visibly imply different fish presence and different reasons to fish
there.

## Fishing is readable fieldcraft

The player learns through fishing conditions: micro-habitat, time of day, and
rig or presentation. Those conditions must create visible, testable hypotheses,
not hidden catch-table puzzles. Weather initially supplies atmosphere and
expedition texture rather than an additional fish-presence dependency.

Fishing retains its existing physical language: cast, fish sign, bite signal,
hook-set, reeling, yielding, line tension, recovery, surge, landing, and loss.
Remove confusion, not decision. A missed bite, line break, or thrown hook must
give the player evidence about what happened.

## Knowledge, not gear score

Advancement widens the player's choices: a different presentation, a newly
reachable bank, earned trust, or evidence that clarifies a promising condition.
Equipment is technique-specific and modest; it never replaces knowing the home
water.

The field journal is the memory of this learning. It records catches, fish
signs, bites, losses, and their conditions, then lets a player preserve an
optional personal note for a meaningful outing. It is a memory map and
hypothesis tool, not a collection-percentage screen.

## Calm needs contrast

The game avoids combat, hunger, and always-draining survival meters. Its
expedition texture comes from travel, passing time, temporary inconvenience,
and fishing risk. Returning to the dock should feel comforting because the
world occasionally asks for attention, not because it exhausts the player.

## The water remains socially present

A local need gives ordinary fishing emotional and practical weight. Three
recurring relationships shape the home community:

- someone directly connected to the local need;
- a practical fishing contact;
- a keeper of local stories or ecological knowledge.

They provide context and changing perspectives, not anonymous fetch tasks. The
watershed mystery unfolds through incomplete and sometimes contradictory local
accounts. Its eventual fish or phenomenon must feel older and stranger than the
progression systems surrounding it.

## The first complete loop

Within 20–30 minutes, a player can receive a local need, choose between two
micro-habitats using visible conditions, land a fish or experience an
instructive loss, record an observation, return to the home community, and
receive the first clue about the watershed mystery. This is the first proof of
the game, not merely a technical fishing demo.
